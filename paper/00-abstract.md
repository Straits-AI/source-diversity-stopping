# Adaptive External Attention: Cost-Efficient Retrieval Through Selective Operation Avoidance Over Heterogeneous Address Spaces

## Abstract

Retrieval-augmented generation systems typically commit to a single retrieval primitive regardless of query characteristics, corpus structure, or operation cost. We formalize retrieval as adaptive external attention allocation over heterogeneous address spaces — semantic, lexical, and entity-graph substrates — modeled as a constrained Markov decision process with explicit discovery/knowledge state tracking and budget constraints. We introduce Adaptive External Attention (AEA), a harness-level routing policy that uses coverage-driven stopping to select among substrates based on workspace state. On HotpotQA Bridge (N=100), AEA achieves a 67% improvement in Utility@Budget over the strongest fixed baseline by completing questions in 1.21 average operations versus 2.00, with comparable support recall (0.795 vs. 0.810). On a controlled heterogeneous benchmark (N=100, 6 task types), AEA matches the best single-substrate policy while achieving higher precision and fewer operations. Ablation analysis reveals a surprising finding: AEA's advantage derives primarily from **routing avoidance** — knowing when to stop and what not to do — rather than from positive substrate selection. Forcing unconditional substrate escalation degrades Utility@Budget by 0.1146, while removing entity-graph hops slightly improves performance. This reframes adaptive retrieval from "choose the right tool" to "know when to stop," with implications for the design of cost-efficient retrieval systems under budget constraints.
