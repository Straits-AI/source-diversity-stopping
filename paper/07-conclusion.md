# 7 Conclusion

We introduced Adaptive External Attention (AEA), a harness-level routing policy over heterogeneous address spaces for retrieval-augmented question answering. The formalization models retrieval as a constrained Markov decision process with explicit discovery and knowledge state tracking, and the policy implements coverage-driven routing that selects among semantic, lexical, and entity-graph substrates based on workspace state rather than query surface patterns.

Our experiments on HotpotQA Bridge and a controlled heterogeneous benchmark demonstrate that AEA achieves competitive or superior Utility@Budget relative to fixed retrieval baselines, with the primary advantage driven by cost efficiency — completing retrieval in fewer operations without sacrificing recall. The ablation study isolates the mechanism: **selective routing avoidance** — the ability to recognize when the current evidence is sufficient and to refrain from unnecessary operations — accounts for the dominant share of AEA's advantage.

This finding reframes the problem of adaptive retrieval. The prevailing intuition is that an adaptive system should learn to choose the right retrieval substrate for each query. Our evidence suggests that, at least for heuristic policies operating under budget constraints, the greater value lies in learning what *not* to do. A policy that defaults to the cheapest available operation and stops when coverage is sufficient outperforms policies that aggressively pursue comprehensive evidence gathering.

Three implications follow for the design of retrieval systems. First, cost-aware evaluation metrics such as Utility@Budget are essential for revealing the true performance profile of retrieval policies; metrics that reward recall without penalizing cost systematically favor wasteful strategies. Second, the null action — the decision not to retrieve — should be treated as a first-class option in any routing framework, not as a fallback. Third, the gap between routing avoidance (where heuristic policies already succeed) and positive routing (where they do not yet reliably outperform single-substrate baselines) defines a clear target for learned policies trained on trajectory-level supervision.

The formalization, benchmark, and empirical finding reported here provide the foundation for a research program in cost-efficient adaptive retrieval. The central question this work leaves open — whether learned routing policies can capture the positive substrate selection that heuristic policies miss — is the natural next step.
